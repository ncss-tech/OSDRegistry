GEOGRAPHICALLY ASSOCIATED SOILS:
These are the Apalachicola, Dry Bar, Eastpoint, McKissack, Norman, Sandbank, and Slough soils.
Apalachicola soils--Formed on lagoon and bay bottoms from fine-loamy lagoonal deposits and have up to 35 percent shell fragments in the particle-size control section.
Dry Bar soils--Formed on shoals and oyster reefs from sandy lagoonal deposits and have a weighted average of 35 percent or more shell fragments in the particle-size control section.
Eastpoint soils--formed on submerged wave-cut platforms from sandy lagoonal materials.
McKissack soils--Formed in brackish estuarine tidal stream from eroded, sandy spodic material.
Norman soils--Formed on shoals and oyster reefs from loamy lagoonal deposits and have a weighted average of 35 percent or more shell fragments in the particle size control section.
Sandbank soils--Formed on estuarine tidal stream deltas and from sandy estuarine deposits and have mica present in some part of the profile.
Slough soils--Formed in brackish estuarine tidal streams from fine-loamy estuarine deposits.

DRAINAGE AND SATURATED HYDRAULIC CONDUCTIVITY:
Drainage class--Subaqueous
Saturated hydraulic conductivity--High to very high
Soil moisture regime--Peraquic
Soil is continuously inundated by brackish or salt water

USE AND VEGETATION:
Major uses--The area is intermittently used for recreational fishing. Sparse, rooted vascular vegetation has been observed on Dregs soils.
Typical vegetation--Subaquatic vegetation is  typically absent, but limited stands of shoal grass (Halodule wrightii) may establish where conditions are permissible.

REMARKS:
Typical Pedon Taxonomic Features:
Human-transported material--The zone from 0 to 202 centimeters.
Reduced matrix--The zone from 123 to 202 centimeters has a soil color matrix with a chroma of 2 or less.
Peraquic feature--Zero or positive soil water potential at the soil surface (permanently submerged).
Taxonomic Version: Keys to Soil Taxonomy, Thirteenth Edition, 2022
Other: The series is named after the term dregs, a term used to describe the sediment which settles to the bottom of liquids after processing. Dregs soils were previously mapped as water in Florida soil surveys.
