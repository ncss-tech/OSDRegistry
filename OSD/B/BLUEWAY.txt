GEOGRAPHICALLY ASSOCIATED SOILS:
These are the Wimico and Slough soils.
Wimico soils--Are in freshwater lakes, formed in lacustrine deposits, and have a color value, moist, of 3 or less and a color value, dry, of 5 or less throughout the upper 18 centimeters of the mineral soil. 
Slough soils--Are in brackish estuarine tidal streams and have 1:5 electrical conductivity greater than 0.6 dS/m somewhere throughout the top 100 centimeters.

DRAINAGE AND SATURATED HYDRAULIC CONDUCTIVITY:
Drainage class--Subaqueous
Saturated hydraulic conductivity--Moderately high to high
Soil moisture regime--Peraquic
Soil is continuously inundated by freshwater and occasionally brackish water in times of drought.

USE AND VEGETATION:
Major uses--The area is used for recreational fishing, boating, and limited residential houseboat anchorages. Blueway soils were historically used for riverine transport of goods. Lower energy Blueway soils are important habitat for crustaceans and juvenile aquatic species. 
Typical vegetation--Sub-aquatic vegetation is generally not observed because of continuous, turbid fluvial flow. Some shallow water soils in less turbid environments support tape grass (Vallisneria americana).

REMARKS:
Typical Pedon Taxonomic Features:
Fluidity--The zone from 0 to 118 centimeters and 195 to 219 centimeters has a fluidity class of moderately fluid to very fluid.
Peraquic feature--Zero or positive soil water potential at the soil surface (permanently submerged).
Soil temperature regime--Blueway and Wimico soils straddle the boundary between both thermic and hyperthermic temperature regimes.  Both soils would be best interpreted as hyperthermic soils, aligning with the nearby and neighboring landscapes and soils (soil correlation decision Special Projects 2026).
Taxonomic Version: Keys to Soil Taxonomy, Thirteenth Edition, 2022
Other: The series is named after the Blueway paddling trails in Florida, designed to increase tourism in historic, scenic waterways throughout the state. Blueway soils were previously mapped as water in Florida soil surveys.
